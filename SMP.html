Purpose of this document
The Technology Resilience Score™ is not a marketing add-on — it is the entry point to every client conversation, the justification for every project, and the anchor for long-term client growth. Every marketing touchpoint, sales call, and client report must reinforce the same message: your score tells you where you are, your roadmap shows where you can go, and LBT gets you there. Brand Language Rules — Non-Negotiable

All consultants and marketing materials must follow these rules. There are no exceptions.

Rule 1 — Always connect the score to a business risk

❌ Never say
✅ Always say
IAM maturity is low.
Your score is being impacted by weaknesses in access control, increasing the likelihood of unauthorised access and credential-based attacks.
You scored 1 on backup — that's very bad.
Your current backup position represents one of the highest areas of operational risk we identified. Without a tested, immutable backup strategy, a ransomware incident could result in permanent data loss and extended business downtime.
Most of our clients are better than you at this.
Most organisations of your size operate at 3.5+. Your current position of 2.3 presents elevated operational risk relative to your peers.
You need to fix your MFA urgently.
Phase 1 of your resilience programme is focused on lifting your score above 3.0 — the risk stabilisation threshold — by addressing the three highest-impact areas identified in this assessment.
This will cost £X per month.
This investment supports your ability to scale, retain clients, and meet supplier and regulatory expectations.
We found a number of issues with your IT security.
Your Technology Resilience Score provides a clear baseline of your current environment and highlights a strong opportunity to enhance resilience, reduce risk, and support future growth.

Rule 2 — Always pair a score with an opportunity

Ambitious clients don't respond to "you're at risk." They respond to "you have a clear path to becoming stronger, more scalable, and more resilient — and we can get you there."

Rule 3 — Always reference trajectory

"You've improved from 2.5 to 3.2 — demonstrating measurable progress in strengthening your operational resilience and significantly reducing your exposure to the most common causes of downtime and breach at your scale."

Stage 1 — Lite Assessment Follow-Up Call

Trigger: Prospect completes Lite Assessment on website  |  Goal: Book a 30-minute call within 24 hours  |  Target: Same day if possible

Opening Line

Opening
"Hi [Name], I've had a look at your Technology Resilience Score from your assessment — you came in at [X.X], and I wanted to pick up the phone because there are a couple of things in there that I think are worth a conversation."

Bridge to Risk

Bridge
"Most organisations of your size in [sector] operate at around 3.5. Your score of [X.X] is below that benchmark — particularly in [top 2 domains]. That's not unusual, but it does mean there are some specific areas where your exposure is higher than it needs to be."

Call to Action

CTA
"What I'd love to do is spend 30 minutes walking you through the detail — not a sales pitch, just an honest look at what the numbers mean for your business and what good would look like. Would [day/time] work?"

Objection Handling

❓ "We already have IT support"
"That's great — this isn't about replacing your IT team, it's about having an independent baseline. Even well-supported businesses often have blind spots. Your score can tell us whether the investment you're making is delivering the resilience you need."
❓ "We've not had any problems"
"That's the most common thing we hear from businesses before an incident. The average dwell time for an attacker is 207 days — the problem often exists long before it's visible. This assessment gives you a view before something goes wrong, not after."
❓ "We can't afford it right now"
"I understand — the assessment is actually the affordable part. It's designed to show you where the highest-impact, lowest-cost improvements are. We typically find businesses are spending money in the wrong places and missing the areas that carry the most risk."
