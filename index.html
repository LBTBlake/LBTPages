What the paid assessment is

The LBT paid assessment is a structured, evidence-based review of a client or prospect's technology and cyber security environment. It is conducted by a trained LBT consultant over a 3–4 hour session, with direct access to the client's admin consoles, policies, and documentation.

It produces a Technology Resilience Score™ — a single number between 1.0 and 5.0 — across ten domains, based on what the consultant actually observed, not what the client told us. Every score above 2 requires observable evidence gathered during the session.

ℹ️
Key distinction: verified, not self-reported

The paid assessment is fundamentally different from the lite (self-service) assessment. Lite scores are what the client thinks they have. Paid scores are what we can evidence they actually have. Only paid and ongoing assessment scores are written to ConnectWise company custom fields.

The paid assessment is what LBT sells as a commercial engagement. It is the evidence base for the managed service proposal — the risk register, phased roadmap, and investment narrative in the proposal all pull directly from the assessment data via ConnectWise Sell tokens.

Who can run it

The paid assessment must be conducted by an LBT consultant who has read and understood the Consultant Scoring Playbook. For your first six assessments, a second consultant must peer-review the scores before the report is issued.

⚠️
Do not run a paid assessment without reading the scoring playbook first

Inconsistent scoring undermines the commercial credibility of the Technology Resilience Score™. An inflated score harms the client by understating risk and weakens the LBT proposal. When in doubt, score lower.

What it produces

  • A Technology Resilience Score™ (overall and per domain)
  • A consultant-verified risk register with rated entries
  • A phased action roadmap (Phase 1 / 2 / 3) auto-generated from scores
  • A client-facing PDF report
  • Score data written to ConnectWise company custom fields (via Rewst)
  • A draft managed service proposal auto-populated in ConnectWise Sell
  • Sales team notification with score summary and top risks

Scoring in the Tool
👤 Owner: Nick HaleyPaid Assessment

How the score is calculated

Each control is scored 1–5. Controls within a domain are averaged to produce a domain score. Domain scores are combined using weighted averages (each domain has a set weight — Cyber Security Controls, for example, carries 14% of the overall score) to produce the overall Technology Resilience Score™.

ℹ️
Opt-out controls are excluded from the calculation

Controls marked N/A or Deferred are excluded. The domain weight is redistributed across the remaining scored controls. Third-Party controls are included at their scored value but capped at 4.0. Risk Accepted controls are forced to 2.0 regardless of compensating controls.

Score scale — quick reference

ScoreLabelWhat it meansFramework threshold
1 — InitialInitialNothing in place or completely ineffective. No evidence possible.No framework satisfied
2 — DevelopingDevelopingSomething exists but not enforced, tested, monitored, or complete. False confidence is common here.No framework satisfied
3 — DefinedDefinedWorking, documented, actively used control. Good coverage, tested infrequently.Cyber Essentials baseline
4 — ManagedManagedFully deployed, monitored, tested, and reported. What LBT's managed service delivers.CE+, ISO 27001 (most controls)
5 — OptimisedOptimisedAutomated, continuously improving, integrated into governance, evidenced for audit.CE+, ISO 27001, SOC 2, NIST CSF

The golden rule

⚠️
If you cannot see it, you cannot score it above 2

A score of 3 or above requires you to have observed, tested, or been shown documented evidence during the session. Client reassurance is not evidence. "They told me it's in place" is a score 2.

⚠️
Before applying an N/A, ask: does the underlying risk still exist?

If the answer is yes — even partially — the control is not N/A. It may be Third-Party or Risk Accepted, but not N/A. A client in a serviced office still has a network security risk — it is managed by their landlord (Third-Party), not absent.

Common N/A scenarios pre-configured in the tool

  • Network security controls — automatically suggested as N/A for 100% WFH clients; suggested as Third-Party for serviced office clients
  • On-premise server management — suggested as N/A when "no on-prem servers" is ticked in Setup
  • Physical access controls — suggested as N/A for 100% WFH; Third-Party for serviced office
  • Mobile MDM — suggested as N/A when "no company-owned mobile devices" is ticked and no corporate data is accessed on personal devices
  • SBOM/bespoke software — suggested as N/A when "no bespoke software" is ticked

Platform Differences — M365 vs Google Workspace
👤 Owner: Nick HaleyPaid Assessment

How platform selection works in the tool

When you select M365 or Google Workspace in the Setup tab, the Assessment tab shows platform-specific guidance inside each control — the correct admin console path, what to check, scoring thresholds specific to that platform, and evidence to capture.

Critical Google Workspace traps

🚨
Google Vault is NOT a backup

Google Vault is a legal hold and eDiscovery tool. It does not protect against data deleted by a user, admin error, or ransomware affecting cloud sync. If a client believes Vault is their backup, score backup at 1 until Axient 360 or equivalent is confirmed.

🚨
Google default audit log retention is 6 months — not 12

The 12-month minimum required for a score of 3 on Monitoring/Logging is NOT met by Google's default. Always confirm extended retention is configured via Vault export or SIEM ingestion. Failure to do this caps the logging score at 2.

⚠️
Google has no native Privileged Identity Management (PIM)

Unlike Entra PIM in M365, Google Workspace has no built-in JIT privileged access management. Score 4–5 on PAM for Google clients requires demonstrable compensating controls: minimal super admin count (≤2 dedicated accounts), Titan Keys or FIDO2 enforced, ConnectWise PAM for all privileged tasks, and a documented compensating control programme. Always note this in the report.

Evidence Capture
👤 Owner: Nick HaleyPaid Assessment

Why evidence capture matters

Evidence is what separates a verified Technology Resilience Score™ from a self-reported guess. Every score of 3 or above must have at least one documented evidence item. Evidence protects LBT professionally, substantiates the risk register in the proposal, and creates an auditable record that scores can be compared against at the next assessment.

What to capture for each control

The evidence capture fields in the tool are:

  • Tool / Platform in use — the specific product or system (e.g. "Huntress", "Axient 360", "Entra ID")
  • Policy / Document reference — filename, SharePoint path, or description of the document reviewed
  • Date last reviewed — when the policy, test, or review last took place. This is critical — see the Recency Trap.
  • Consultant Notes — what you actually saw: config screenshots, specific settings observed, exclusions noted, context that explains the score
  • Risk Acceptance Document Location — (shown when Risk Accepted opt-out is applied) — where the signed acceptance is filed
  • Third-Party Evidence Reviewed — (shown when Third-Party opt-out is applied) — what evidence of the third party's controls was reviewed (ISO cert, SLA, policy document)

Evidence folder structure

Create the folder before the session starts:

Clients / [Client Name] / Assessment / [YYYY-MM-DD] /

Within it, save:

  • Screenshots of admin console configurations (named by control: e.g. iam_mfa_conditional_access.png)
  • Policy documents reviewed (PDFs or Word docs)
  • Exported reports (patch compliance, device enrolment, backup job logs)
  • Signed risk acceptance documents (for any Risk Accepted opt-outs)
💡
Name screenshots with the control ID

Using the control ID as the filename (e.g. cy_email_dmarc_record.png) makes it easy to find evidence at the next assessment and link it to specific controls in a peer review.

QBR Mode
Overview & Purpose
👤 Owner: Nick Haley QBR Mode

What the QBR mode is

QBR mode is the quarterly touchpoint that turns the initial paid assessment into a long-term client relationship engine. It is not simply a re-run of the assessment — it is a structured conversation about progress, priorities, and the next quarter's investment.

It runs in the same assessment tool as the paid assessment. Switch to QBR mode using the mode bar at the top of the screen.

What QBR mode does

  • Compares the current quarter's scores against the previous quarter (delta view)
  • Surfaces which domains have improved, stayed flat, or regressed
  • Takes a steer from the client on what they care about most right now (the Priority selector)
  • Generates a prioritised action plan using the scoring engine — ranked by greatest overall score gain, weighted by client priorities, with floor domains always first
  • Produces a QBR report slide suitable for the meeting itself

The prioritisation engine — how it works

The engine ranks actions using five factors:

  1. Floor-first rule — any domain scoring below 2.0 is automatically ranked first, regardless of priorities. You cannot push a 3 to a 4 while a 1 exists anywhere in the environment.
  2. Domain balance — domains below the current overall average are boosted; domains already above average are dampened. This prevents polishing one area while neglecting others.
  3. Client priority weighting — the priorities selected by the client apply multipliers to relevant domains (e.g. selecting "Cyber Insurance" boosts IAM, Cyber, and BCDR).
  4. Effort efficiency — lower-effort actions score higher than equivalent-gain heavy lifts. Quick wins surface naturally.
  5. Benchmark gap — domains below the SMB benchmark of 3.5 receive an additional 1.3× boost.
✅
The engine shows the overall score impact of each individual action

Every action in the plan shows exactly how many points it would add to the client's overall Technology Resilience Score™ if completed. The consultant can show the client a running projection: "If we complete these three actions next quarter, your score moves from 2.8 to 3.3."

Setting Client Priorities
👤 Owner: Nick HaleyQBR Mode

What the priority selector does

Client priorities adjust the weighting of the action plan engine. Selecting a priority applies domain multipliers — domains directly relevant to that priority are ranked higher in the action plan. This allows the action plan to reflect what the client cares about commercially right now, not just the abstract technical priority order.

ℹ️
Floor-first always overrides priorities

No matter which priorities are selected, any domain scoring below 2.0 is ranked first in the action plan. A client who says "I only care about compliance" cannot deprioritise a critical floor domain — the engine will not allow it.

The best time to set priorities is at the start of the QBR meeting, after you have shared the score overview. Ask the client directly:

QBR Mode
The Action Plan Engine
👤 Owner: Nick HaleyQBR Mode

Reading the action plan

Each action card in the Action Plan tab shows:

  • Rank number — priority order. 🚨 Critical Floor badge means this domain is below 2.0 and is ranked first regardless of priorities.
  • Action label — what needs to be done to move the domain to the next score level
  • Domain and score move — which domain this addresses and the specific score move (e.g. IAM: 1.9 → 2.0)
  • Domain gain — how many points this adds to that domain's score
  • Overall lift — how many points this action adds to the overall Technology Resilience Score™
  • After this action — what the overall score becomes if only this action is completed
  • Effort rating — ★ to ★★★★ (lower = quicker win)
  • LBT Delivered badge — whether LBT delivers this or whether it is a client-owned action
  • Running progress bar — shows visually where the overall score sits after completing each successive action

Using the projection summary

At the bottom of the Action Plan tab, a projection panel shows the overall score after completing the top 3, top 6, and all actions. Use this in the meeting to frame the next quarter's ambition:

"If we focus on just the top three actions this quarter, your score moves from 2.8 to 3.3. That takes you above the risk stabilisation threshold and is the most impactful use of our time together over the next 90 days."

Marking actions complete

As work is completed between QBRs, the consultant can mark actions done using the Mark done button on each card. Completed actions are shown with a green ✓ and greyed out. They remain visible so the client can see progress. The projection updates in real time.

Why the floor rule matters — a practical example

⚠️
Scenario: client wants to focus on compliance (ISO 27001 target)

A client scores 3.2 on Governance and 1.6 on BCDR (backup/recovery). They say they want to focus on compliance and governance this quarter. The compliance priority multiplier boosts Governance — but BCDR is at 1.6, below the 2.0 floor threshold. The engine ranks the BCDR action first regardless. The consultant explains: "Your backup position presents an immediate and existential risk — a ransomware attack today would be catastrophic. We need to address this before we can build the governance layer on top of it." This is not a negotiable ranking.

What the QBR report contains

The QBR Report tab produces a client-facing document containing:

  • Score header — last quarter vs this quarter, score change, and projected target
  • Domain progress grid — all 10 domains with score bars and delta arrows (▲ improved / ▼ regressed / → unchanged)
  • Quarter summary — which domains improved, maintained, or need attention; auto-generated narrative paragraph
  • Priority actions table — the top 5 actions from the prioritised plan, with domain, score move, overall lift, effort, and whether LBT or client delivers
  • Score trajectory — four milestones: last quarter → this quarter → top 3 actions complete → full programme

Sharing the QBR report

The report is designed to be screenshot or printed directly from the QBR Report tab. In production, clicking Generate QBR Report will trigger the Rewst workflow to produce a branded PDF and email it to the client contact within 48 hours of the meeting.

💡
Show the report in the meeting, not just after it

Switching to the QBR Report tab in the last 10 minutes of the meeting and showing the client the trajectory section is one of the most effective moments in the QBR. "Here's where you were, here's where you are, and here's where we can take you." It makes the conversation tangible and the value of the ongoing relationship visible.

Upsell opportunities surfaced by QBR mode

The action plan naturally surfaces upsell conversations. LBT-delivered actions that appear in the top 3 are direct service upsell opportunities. Look for:

  • Actions marked "LBT Delivered" that the client doesn't yet have — these are new service line conversations
  • Domains that have stalled at 2.x for two consecutive quarters — there is likely a barrier (cost, awareness, or technical dependency) to address
  • Trajectory conversations — "To reach 4.0, which is where you need to be for CE+ / your insurance / enterprise clients, we need to add [service]. Here's what that looks like."

Report Language Guide
👤 Owner: Nick HaleyBoth

The three rules

  1. Always pair a score with the business risk it represents — never state a score in isolation
  2. Always reference trajectory — where they are going, not just where they are now
  3. Always link to business outcomes: growth, client confidence, regulatory standing, insurability — not just security
